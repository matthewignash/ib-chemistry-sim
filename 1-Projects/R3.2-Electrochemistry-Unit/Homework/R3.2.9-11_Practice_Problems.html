1
Multiple Choice [1 mark]
Pentan-3-ol is heated under reflux with an excess of acidified potassium dichromate(VI). Which row correctly identifies the organic product and the color change observed?
  • A. Pentanal; orange to green
  • B. Pentan-3-one; orange to green
  • C. Pentanoic acid; orange to green
  • D. Pentan-3-one; orange to purple

Answer: B

Pentan-3-ol is a secondary alcohol (the —OH is on carbon 3, which is bonded to two other carbons). Oxidation of a secondary alcohol produces a ketone — specifically pentan-3-one. The acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ changes from orange (Cr₂O₇²⁻, Cr(VI)) to green (Cr³⁺, Cr(III)).

  • A is wrong: pentanal is an aldehyde, which would come from a primary alcohol (pentan-1-ol)
  • C is wrong: pentanoic acid is a carboxylic acid from full oxidation of a primary alcohol — secondary alcohols stop at the ketone
  • D is wrong: the product is correct but the color change is wrong — dichromate goes orange→green, not orange→purple (purple is associated with permanganate, KMnO₄)

3
Multiple Choice [1 mark]
But-1-ene undergoes hydrogenation. Which statement about this reaction is correct?
  • A. The product is but-1-yne and the reaction is an oxidation
  • B. The product is butane and the reaction requires UV light as a catalyst
  • C. The product is butane and the reaction requires a nickel catalyst and heat
  • D. The product is butan-1-ol and the reaction requires a nickel catalyst

Answer: C

Hydrogenation adds H₂ across the C=C double bond: CH₂=CHCH₂CH₃ + H₂ → CH₃CH₂CH₂CH₃. The product is butane (the alkane). Standard conditions are Ni catalyst (or Pt/Pd) at approximately 150°C.

  • A is wrong: but-1-yne has a triple bond (fewer H atoms, not more) — hydrogenation adds H₂, not removes it
  • B is wrong: the product is correct but UV light is the condition for free-radical substitution of alkanes, not hydrogenation
  • D is wrong: butan-1-ol would require hydration (addition of H₂O, not H₂), typically using H₃PO₄ catalyst

Three unlabeled bottles each contain one of the following: ethanol (a primary alcohol), propan-2-ol (a secondary alcohol), and 2-methylpropan-2-ol (a tertiary alcohol). A student adds acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ to each and heats.

Write your answer here...

Marking Scheme

(a)

  • Ethanol (primary): solution changes from orange to green — ethanol is oxidized to ethanal/ethanoic acid. [½]
  • Propan-2-ol (secondary): solution changes from orange to green — propan-2-ol is oxidized to propanone. [½]
  • 2-methylpropan-2-ol (tertiary): no color change — the solution remains orange because tertiary alcohols cannot be oxidized by dichromate. [1]

(b) Any one of the following [1]:

  • Add Tollens' reagent (ammoniacal silver nitrate): the primary alcohol product (an aldehyde) gives a silver mirror; the secondary alcohol product (a ketone) does not react.
  • Add Fehling's/Benedict's solution and heat: the aldehyde produces a red/orange precipitate (Cu₂O); the ketone shows no change.
  • Add more acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ under reflux: the aldehyde is further oxidized (more green color); the ketone is not further oxidized.

Marking Scheme

(a)

C₆H₁₀ + H₂ → Ni, ~150°C C₆H₁₂

Accept: cyclohexene + H₂ → cyclohexane, with Ni (or Pt or Pd) catalyst and heat stated. [1]

(b) The carbon atoms involved in the double bond gain hydrogen atoms. Gaining hydrogen (or gaining electrons, or decreasing in oxidation state) is the definition of reduction. The average oxidation state of carbon decreases from −(10/6) in C₆H₁₀ to −2 in C₆H₁₂. [1]

(c) Any one of: [1]

  • Cyclohexene decolorizes bromine water; cyclohexane does not (chemical test for unsaturation)
  • Cyclohexene has a slightly higher boiling point than cyclohexane (due to stronger intermolecular forces from the planar double bond region) — though this is subtle and either order is acceptable if justified
  • Cyclohexene reacts with KMnO₄ (turns purple to colorless/brown); cyclohexane does not
