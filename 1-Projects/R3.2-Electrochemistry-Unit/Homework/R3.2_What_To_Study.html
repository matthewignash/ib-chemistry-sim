2. Key Vocabulary

Oxidation
Loss of electrons; increase in oxidation state
Reduction
Gain of electrons; decrease in oxidation state
Redox reaction
A reaction where both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously
Oxidizing agent
Substance that is reduced; accepts electrons
Reducing agent
Substance that is oxidized; donates electrons
Voltaic cell
Electrochemical cell that produces electrical energy from spontaneous redox reactions
Electrolytic cell
Electrochemical cell that requires electrical energy to drive a non-spontaneous reaction
Anode
Electrode where oxidation occurs (negative in voltaic; positive in electrolytic)
Cathode
Electrode where reduction occurs (positive in voltaic; negative in electrolytic)
Salt bridge
Contains inert ions; allows ion flow to maintain charge neutrality in voltaic cells
Half-equation
Equation showing only oxidation or only reduction in a redox process
Cell notation
Shorthand representation of a voltaic cell: anode | solution || solution | cathode
Primary cell
Non-rechargeable battery; irreversible reactions
Secondary cell
Rechargeable battery; reversible reactions (e.g., Li-ion, lead-acid)
Electrolysis
Use of electrical current to drive non-spontaneous chemical reactions
Spontaneous reaction
Reaction that occurs naturally without external energy input (ΔG < 0)

3. Common Exam Mistakes (Avoid These!)

Mistake 1: Confusing anode and cathode polarity

The error: Saying the cathode is always negative and anode always positive (this is true for electrolytic cells but NOT for voltaic cells)
Why it happens: Students learn this rule from electrolysis first and apply it everywhere
The fix: Remember: In voltaic cells, cathode = positive, anode = negative. In electrolytic cells, cathode = negative, anode = positive. The definition is the same (anode = oxidation, cathode = reduction) but the polarity is opposite!

Mistake 2: Writing half-equations that don't balance

The error: Forgetting to balance electrons, atoms, or charge in half-equations
Why it happens: Students rush and don't check their work systematically
The fix: Always check: (1) atom balance, (2) charge balance, (3) electron count. Write electrons last. Make sure electrons are equal when combining half-equations.

Mistake 3: Wrong direction of ion movement in electrolytic cells

The error: Drawing anions moving toward the cathode or cations moving toward the anode
Why it happens: Confusion about which ions are positive/negative
The fix: Cations (positive ions) always move toward cathode. Anions (negative ions) always move toward anode. Think: opposite charges attract.

Mistake 4: Assuming electrolysis always produces the same products

The error: Predicting O₂ and H₂ from water without checking concentration and electrode material
Why it happens: Students memorize "water gives H₂ and O₂" but ignore context
The fix: Check: Is it molten or aqueous? What ions are present? What are the relative concentrations? Product prediction depends on these factors.

Mistake 5: Confusing discharge and charge reactions in rechargeable cells

The error: Writing the same half-equations for both discharge and charge, or reversing them incorrectly
Why it happens: Students don't understand that charging reverses the discharge reaction
The fix: Discharge and charge reactions are opposites. If discharge is A → B, then charge is B → A. Electrons flow in opposite directions too.

Mistake 6: Forgetting what the salt bridge does

The error: Saying the salt bridge conducts electrons (it doesn't!) or forgetting to mention charge balance
Why it happens: Confusion between electron flow and ion flow
The fix: The salt bridge allows ion flow only (not electrons). Purpose: prevent charge buildup and maintain electrical neutrality in both compartments.

5. Practice Priority — Where to Focus Study Time

IB question bank analysis shows these topics appear most frequently in exams. Allocate study time accordingly.

Highest Priority (30-35% of exam questions):
  • Writing and balancing half-equations for redox reactions
  • Identifying oxidation states and oxidizing/reducing agents
  • Predicting electrolysis products in molten salts and aqueous solutions
  • Voltaic cell components and electron/ion flow
★★ Medium Priority (25-30% of questions):
• Cell notation and voltaic cell diagrams
• Reactivity series and displacement reactions
• Li-ion and rechargeable battery reactions
• Charge and discharge half-equations
★ Lower Priority (15-20% of questions):
• Salt bridge function and design
• Primary vs. secondary cell comparison
• Non-standard electrode potentials (HL only)
• Faraday calculations (appears less often in R3.2 assessments)
Study Strategy: Complete all "Highest Priority" topics first until you can answer questions confidently. Then move to "Medium" and "Lower" priority. Revisit high-priority topics regularly throughout your study.
