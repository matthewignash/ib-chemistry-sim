Multiple Choice Questions - Answer Key

Detailed answers with explanations for marking and feedback.
Question 1 (Reactivity Series)
Which of the following reactions is spontaneous?
A. Cd²⁺(aq) + Pb(s) → Cd(s) + Pb²⁺(aq)
B. Fe²⁺(aq) + Sn(s) → Fe(s) + Sn²⁺(aq)
C. Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s) → Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq)
D. Ni²⁺(aq) + Co(s) → Ni(s) + Co²⁺(aq)

Correct Answer: D

Why D is correct: For a reaction to be spontaneous, the solid metal must be more reactive than the metal ion in solution. This means the metal must be able to displace the other metal from its salt. In option D, Co is more reactive than Ni, so it can displace Ni²⁺.
Step-by-step Reasoning: 1. Check the reactivity series: Ni > Co in the standard series? No—Co > Ni
2. If Co > Ni in reactivity, then Co will displace Ni²⁺
3. The reaction will proceed spontaneously (ΔG < 0)
Common Mistakes: • Students may confuse the direction of the reactivity series
• Confusing which metal is the "more reactive" based on the equation format
• Assuming all metal displacement reactions are spontaneous
Concept Tested: Application of the reactivity series to predict spontaneous reactions and identify which species is oxidized/reduced.
Exam Technique: Always refer to the reactivity series. If the solid metal is more reactive than the ion's metal, the reaction is spontaneous.
Question 2 (Oxidation States)
In which compound does sulfur have an oxidation state of +4?
A. H₂S
B. SO₃
C. H₂SO₃
D. H₂SO₄

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct: In H₂SO₃ (sulfurous acid), sulfur has an oxidation state of +4.
Oxidation State Calculation: H₂SO₃: Let S oxidation state = x
2(+1) + x + 3(−2) = 0
2 + x − 6 = 0
x = +4 ✓
Why other options are wrong: • A: H₂S → S = −2
• B: SO₃ → S = +6
• D: H₂SO₄ → S = +6
Concept Tested: Assignment of oxidation states using systematic rules. Understanding that sulfur can have multiple oxidation states.
Exam Technique: Always apply oxidation state rules systematically. Remember: H = +1, O = −2 (except in peroxides). Let the sum equal the charge.
Question 3 (Voltaic Cell)
In a voltaic cell containing Ag and Mg electrodes, what are the sign and electrode type for the silver electrode?
A. Positive cathode
B. Positive anode
C. Negative cathode
D. Negative anode

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct: Silver is less reactive than magnesium, so Ag is the cathode (where reduction occurs). The cathode in a voltaic cell is positive.
Step-by-step Reasoning: 1. Mg is more reactive than Ag (from the reactivity series)
2. More reactive metal = anode (oxidized)
3. Less reactive metal = cathode (reduced)
4. Therefore: Ag = cathode (positive in voltaic cell)
Common Mistakes: • Confusing anode/cathode with positive/negative
• Forgetting that voltaic cells have positive cathode (not like electrolytic cells)
• Assigning electrodes backwards based on metal names
Concept Tested: Understanding voltaic cell structure, identifying electrodes based on reactivity, and electrode polarity in electrochemical cells.
Exam Technique: Remember: In voltaic cells, cathode = positive, anode = negative. The more reactive metal is always the anode.
Question 4 (Reactivity Order)
Tin is able to displace lead from its salt, but is unable to displace manganese. What is the order of decreasing reactivity?
A. Pb > Sn > Mn
B. Sn > Pb > Mn
C. Mn > Sn > Pb
D. Mn > Pb > Sn

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct: From the given information: Sn can displace Pb (so Sn > Pb), and Sn cannot displace Mn (so Mn > Sn). Therefore: Mn > Sn > Pb.
Deduction from Evidence: • Sn + Pb²⁺ → reaction occurs → Sn is more reactive than Pb
• Sn + Mn²⁺ → no reaction → Mn is more reactive than Sn
• Combining: Mn > Sn > Pb
Common Mistakes: • Reversing the logic of displacement reactions
• Placing Pb > Sn (backwards)
• Assuming the middle element is most reactive
Concept Tested: Deduction of reactivity series from displacement reaction evidence. Understanding that more reactive metals displace less reactive ones.
Exam Technique: If A displaces B, then A > B in reactivity. Use this logic to order metals when given displacement data.
Question 5 (Electrolysis)
What are the products of the electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃?
A. O₂ at anode, Al at cathode
B. O₂ at cathode, Al at anode
C. Al₂ at anode, O at cathode
D. Al at anode, O₂ at cathode

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct: In the electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃:
• Anode (oxidation): 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
• Cathode (reduction): Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
Half-equations at each electrode: Cathode (reduction): Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
Anode (oxidation): 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
Common Mistakes: • Swapping anode and cathode products
• Producing "Al₂" or "O" as products instead of elemental forms
• Confusing oxidation and reduction at each electrode
Concept Tested: Predicting electrolysis products in molten salts. Recognizing what is oxidized and reduced. Understanding the Hall-Héroult process for aluminium extraction.
Exam Technique: At the cathode, cations are reduced (gain electrons). At the anode, anions are oxidized (lose electrons). Products are elemental forms.
Question 6 (Oxidizing Agent)
In which reaction does H₂ act as an oxidizing agent?
A. H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
B. 2Na + H₂ → 2NaH
C. N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃
D. C + H₂O → CO + H₂

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct: In 2Na + H₂ → 2NaH, hydrogen is the oxidizing agent. H goes from 0 to −1 (reduction), meaning H₂ oxidizes Na (Na: 0 → +1).
Oxidation State Changes in Option B: Na: 0 → +1 (oxidized)
H: 0 → −1 (reduced)
Since H is reduced, H₂ is the oxidizing agent.
Why other options are wrong: • A: H₂ is the reducing agent (0 → +1)
• C: H₂ is the reducing agent (0 → +1)
• D: H₂O is the oxidizing agent, not H₂
Concept Tested: Identifying oxidizing and reducing agents. Understanding that oxidizing agents are reduced (gain electrons) and reducing agents are oxidized (lose electrons).
Exam Technique: Trace oxidation state changes. If H goes down in oxidation state, H₂ is the oxidizing agent. Remember: oxidizing agent = substance that is reduced.
Question 7 (Voltaic Cell Electrode)
In a voltaic cell, metal X and metal Z are connected as electrodes, with Z being more reactive than X. What occurs in this cell?
A. The mass of electrode X decreases
B. The mass of electrode Z increases
C. X is the cathode and gains electrons
D. The mass of electrode Z decreases

Correct Answer: D

Why D is correct: Z is more reactive, so it is the anode and undergoes oxidation (Z → Z^n+ + ne⁻). As Z loses mass to form ions, the electrode mass decreases.
What happens at each electrode: Anode (Z): Z → Z^n+ + ne⁻ (oxidation—mass decreases)
Cathode (X): X^n+ + ne⁻ → X (reduction—mass increases)
Common Mistakes: • Confusing which electrode gains/loses mass
• Forgetting that the anode metal dissolves
• Assuming both electrodes change mass in the same direction
Concept Tested: Understanding mass changes in voltaic cells. Recognizing anode dissolution and cathode deposition.
Exam Technique: More reactive metal = anode = dissolves = mass decreases. Less reactive metal = cathode = deposits = mass increases.

Short Answer Questions - Answers & Marking Guide

Question 8 (Li-ion Battery) 3 marks
(a) Write a half-equation for the reaction at the anode during discharge. 1 mark

Accepted Answers (mark as correct):

Li → Li⁺ + e⁻
or
LiC₆ → C₆ + Li⁺ + e⁻
or
Li → Li⁺ + e⁻ (at graphite electrode)
Why this is correct: Lithium is oxidized at the anode (loses 1 electron), forming Li⁺ ions. The more complete version shows that Li is extracted from the graphite (LiC₆) complex.
Marking Notes: Award 1 mark for correctly showing Li → Li⁺ + e⁻. Both the simple and detailed versions are acceptable at IB level.
(b) The battery is rechargeable. On a diagram, show with an arrow the direction of electron flow in the external circuit during recharging. 1 mark

Correct Response:

During recharging, electrons flow from the power supply THROUGH the external circuit FROM cathode TO anode. This is the opposite direction to discharge.
What to look for in diagram: • Arrow drawn from the cathode electrode to the anode electrode
• Arrow labeled or clearly showing electron flow direction
• Correct identification of which is cathode/anode
Common error: Students often draw the arrow in the discharge direction. Emphasize that recharging reverses the cell reaction, reversing electron flow.
(c) Suggest why solar panels are often connected with Li-ion batteries. 1 mark

Accepted Answers:

• To store surplus/excess energy from solar panels for use when sun is not shining
• Solar panels produce variable power; batteries provide stable supply
• To allow energy use at night or when sunlight is insufficient
Why this is correct: Solar energy is intermittent. Li-ion batteries have good energy storage capacity and rechargeability, making them ideal for storing peak solar energy for later use.
Marking Notes: Award 1 mark for any clear explanation showing understanding that batteries store excess solar energy for later use.

Extended Answer - Answer & Marking Scheme

Question 9 (Electrolytic Cell) 4 marks
(a) On a diagram, label the anode and cathode, and show with arrows the direction of movement of the Fe²⁺ and S²⁻ ions. 2 marks

Expected Diagram & Labels:

Correct Setup:
• Anode (connected to +) on one side
• Cathode (connected to −) on other side
• S²⁻ ions shown with arrow moving toward anode (positive)
• Fe²⁺ ions shown with arrow moving toward cathode (negative)
Why ions move this way: Negative ions (S²⁻, anions) move toward the anode (positive electrode).
Positive ions (Fe²⁺, cations) move toward the cathode (negative electrode).
This is fundamental to understanding ion movement in electrolysis.
Marking Scheme: • 1 mark: Correct labeling of anode and cathode
• 1 mark: Correct arrow directions for both ion types
(b) Write half-equations for the reaction at each electrode. 2 marks

Correct Half-equations:

Anode (oxidation): 2S²⁻ → S₂ + 4e⁻ (or S²⁻ → S + 2e⁻)
Cathode (reduction): Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Fe
Why these equations are correct: • At anode: S²⁻ is oxidized (loses electrons), forming sulfur
• At cathode: Fe²⁺ is reduced (gains electrons), forming metallic iron
• Electrons are balanced in the overall cell equation
Acceptable variations: Anode: 2S²⁻ → S₂ + 4e⁻ (produces disulfide)
or
S²⁻ → S + 2e⁻ (produces individual S atoms)
Marking Scheme: • 1 mark: Correct cathode half-equation
• 1 mark: Correct anode half-equation (either S₂ or S product is acceptable)
