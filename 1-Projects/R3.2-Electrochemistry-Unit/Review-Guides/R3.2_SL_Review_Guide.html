Section 1: Oxidation and Reduction (R3.2.1–R3.2.2)

Key Concepts:
Oxidation is the loss of electrons (or increase in oxidation state). Reduction is the gain of electrons (or decrease in oxidation state). A redox reaction involves both processes simultaneously. Oxidation states follow specific rules: elements = 0, monoatomic ions = charge, O usually −2 (except in peroxides −1), H usually +1 (except in metal hydrides −1), and all oxidation states sum to molecular charge.
Practice Problem 1: Assign oxidation states to all atoms in K₂Cr₂O₇ ▼

Assign oxidation states to all atoms in K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate).

Step 1: Start with what you know. Potassium is a Group 1 metal → oxidation state = +1
Step 2: Oxygen is not in a peroxide → oxidation state = −2
Step 3: Set up an equation. Compound is neutral, so: 2(+1) + 2x + 7(−2) = 0, where x = oxidation state of Cr
Step 4: Solve: +2 + 2x − 14 = 0 → 2x = 12 → x = +6
Step 5: Check: 2(+1) + 2(+6) + 7(−2) = +2 + 12 − 14 = 0 ✓
Answer:
K: +1
Cr: +6
O: −2
Practice Problem 2: Identify oxidized and reduced species ▼

Identify the oxidized and reduced species in this reaction:

2Fe³⁺(aq) + Sn²⁺(aq) → 2Fe²⁺(aq) + Sn⁴⁺(aq)
Step 1: Assign oxidation states to each species:
Fe³⁺ = +3, Sn²⁺ = +2, Fe²⁺ = +2, Sn⁴⁺ = +4
Step 2: Track oxidation state changes:
Fe: +3 → +2 (decrease by 1) = REDUCTION (gains electrons)
Sn: +2 → +4 (increase by 2) = OXIDATION (loses electrons)
Step 3: Identify species by their role:
Oxidized species: The substance that is oxidized (loses electrons) = Sn²⁺
Reduced species: The substance that is reduced (gains electrons) = Fe³⁺
Oxidized species: Sn²⁺ (oxidation state +2 → +4)
Reduced species: Fe³⁺ (oxidation state +3 → +2)
Practice Problem 3: Spontaneity from reactivity series (MCQ) ▼

Which reaction is spontaneous? (Use the reactivity series: K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Cu > Ag)

A) Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2Ag(s)
B) Zn(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Zn²⁺(aq) + Cu(s)
C) Fe(s) + Zn²⁺(aq) → Fe²⁺(aq) + Zn(s)
D) Ag(s) + Cu²⁺(aq) → Ag⁺(aq) + Cu(s)
Correct Answer: B
A spontaneous redox reaction occurs when a more reactive metal reduces the ions of a less reactive metal. Zn is more reactive than Cu, so Zn can reduce Cu²⁺ → spontaneous. ✓

Why others are wrong:
A) Cu is less reactive than Ag - not spontaneous
C) Fe is less reactive than Zn - not spontaneous
D) Ag is less reactive than Cu - not spontaneous
IB Exam Strategy: When asked to assign oxidation states, always show your working step-by-step. For redox reactions, clearly state which species is oxidized (loses electrons/goes to higher oxidation state) and which is reduced (gains electrons/goes to lower oxidation state). Examiners reward clear method. Remember: oxidation and reduction always occur together in the same reaction.

Section 2: Voltaic (Galvanic) Cells (R3.2.3–R3.2.4)

Key Concepts:
Voltaic cells harness spontaneous redox reactions to produce electrical current. Components include two electrodes (anode = where oxidation occurs, cathode = where reduction occurs), an electrolyte in each half-cell, and a salt bridge (allows ions to flow, maintains electrical neutrality). Electrons flow from anode to cathode through external circuit. Cell notation: Anode | Electrolyte || Electrolyte | Cathode
Practice Problem 4: Write cell notation for Zn/Cu cell ▼

For a Zn/Cu voltaic cell with Zn in ZnSO₄ and Cu in CuSO₄, write the cell notation and identify the anode and cathode.

Step 1: Determine which metal is more reactive. Zn is more reactive than Cu (from reactivity series).
Step 2: The more reactive metal is oxidized (loses electrons) → this is the ANODE (negative pole)
Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻
Step 3: The less reactive metal is where reduction occurs → this is the CATHODE (positive pole)
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Step 4: Write cell notation: Anode (−) | Electrolyte || Electrolyte | Cathode (+)
Cell Notation:
Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq) || Cu²⁺(aq) | Cu(s)

Anode: Zn (where oxidation occurs)
Cathode: Cu (where reduction occurs)
Practice Problem 5: Electrode identification (MCQ) ▼

In a voltaic cell, which statement is correct about the electrodes?

A) The anode is positive and reduction occurs there
B) The cathode is negative and oxidation occurs there
C) The anode is where oxidation occurs and it is the negative electrode
D) Electrons flow from cathode to anode through the external circuit
Correct Answer: C
In a voltaic cell, the anode is where oxidation occurs (loss of electrons), making it the negative pole. Electrons are produced here and flow toward the cathode. ✓

Why others are wrong:
A) Anode is negative (not positive) in a voltaic cell; reduction occurs at cathode
B) Cathode is positive (not negative); reduction (not oxidation) occurs there
D) Electrons flow from anode to cathode (not the reverse)
Practice Problem 6: Role of the salt bridge ▼

Explain the role of the salt bridge in a voltaic cell.

Model Answer with Marking Points:

Point 1: The salt bridge connects the two half-cells / allows ionic communication between compartments
Point 2: It maintains electrical neutrality / allows anions to migrate toward the anode and cations toward the cathode
Point 3: Without it, charge imbalances build up and the cell stops working / reaction cannot continue
Point 4: It does NOT allow electrons to pass / electrons only flow through external circuit

Section 3: Electrolysis (R3.2.5)

Key Concepts:
Electrolysis is a non-spontaneous process that uses electrical energy to drive a redox reaction. At the cathode (−), reduction occurs (cations gain electrons). At the anode (+), oxidation occurs (anions lose electrons, or the electrode corrodes). Products depend on electrolyte type: molten salts vs. aqueous solutions. In aqueous solutions, water can be oxidized/reduced if the ions in solution are harder to oxidize/reduce.
Practice Problem 7: Electrolysis of molten NaCl ▼

Write the half-equations for the electrolysis of molten NaCl. Identify the product at each electrode.

Step 1: In molten NaCl, only Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions are present.
Step 2: At the cathode (−), cations are reduced. Na⁺ is present, so:
Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na
Step 3: At the anode (+), anions are oxidized. Cl⁻ is present, so:
2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻
Step 4: Overall reaction: 2Na⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → 2Na + Cl₂ (with electrical energy)
Cathode (reduction): Na⁺ + e⁻ → Na
Anode (oxidation): 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻

Products: Sodium metal (cathode), Chlorine gas (anode)

Practice Problem 9: Electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃ (MCQ) ▼

Which statement is correct about the electrolysis of molten Al₂O₃?

A) Al forms at the anode and O₂ forms at the cathode
B) Al forms at the cathode and O₂ forms at the anode
C) Both Al and O₂ form at the cathode due to displacement
D) Na metal is produced because Al³⁺ cannot be reduced
Correct Answer: B
At the cathode (−), Al³⁺ is reduced: Al³⁺ + 3e⁻ → Al
At the anode (+), O²⁻ is oxidized: 2O²⁻ → O₂ + 4e⁻
This is the basis of the Hall-Héroult process for aluminum production. ✓

Why others are wrong:
A) Products reversed
C) Al is a single product, forms only at cathode
D) Al³⁺ can be reduced if sufficient voltage applied; this is a commercial process

Section 4: Rechargeable Batteries (R3.2.7)

Key Concepts:
Primary cells (non-rechargeable) produce energy through irreversible reactions. Secondary cells (rechargeable) involve reversible reactions; applying external voltage reverses the reaction, restoring the cell. Examples: Lead-acid batteries, Li-ion batteries. The key difference is whether the discharge reactions can be reversed without decomposing the electrode materials.
Practice Problem 10: Li-ion battery charging reaction ▼

During discharge, a Li-ion battery undergoes: Li + CoO₂ → LiCoO₂. Write the equation for the charging reaction.

Step 1: Understand discharge: Li is oxidized (anode), CoO₂ is reduced (cathode), forming LiCoO₂
Step 2: Charging reverses this process. External voltage applies reverse electromotive force.
Step 3: The product of discharge (LiCoO₂) is the reactant of charging.
Step 4: Charging equation is the reverse of discharge:
LiCoO₂ → Li + CoO₂ (with electrical energy input)
Step 5: This restores the original materials, allowing another discharge cycle.
Charging Reaction:
LiCoO₂ → Li + CoO₂

This is the exact reverse of the discharge reaction, which is why Li-ion batteries are rechargeable.
Practice Problem 11: Why lead-acid rechargeable but Zn-C not ▼

Explain why lead-acid batteries can be recharged but zinc-carbon batteries cannot.

Model Answer with Marking Points:

Point 1: Lead-acid batteries undergo reversible reactions: PbO₂ + Pb + 2H₂SO₄ ⇌ 2PbSO₄ + 2H₂O
Point 2: When charged, the reverse reaction occurs, regenerating PbO₂ and Pb electrodes
Point 3: Zinc-carbon batteries involve irreversible reactions where the zinc anode is consumed/corroded
Point 4: The products of discharge cannot be converted back to original reactants by applying reverse voltage
Point 5: Attempting to charge Zn-C cells damages electrodes and can cause leakage
Practice Problem 12: Electron flow during charging ▼

Describe the direction of electron flow during charging of a rechargeable battery and explain how it differs from discharge.

Answer:
During Discharge: Electrons flow spontaneously from the anode (negative pole) → through external circuit → to the cathode (positive pole).

During Charging: An external power source pumps electrons from the cathode to the anode, forcing them to flow in the opposite direction. Electrons are pushed INTO the anode and pulled OUT of the cathode.

Key difference: Discharge is spontaneous (oxidation at anode, reduction at cathode); charging is forced (the polarity effectively reverses at the electrodes, but we still call them anode/cathode based on the discharge reaction).

Mixed Q1: Complete redox analysis (challenging) ▼

Consider the reaction: 2MnO₄⁻(aq) + 5H₂O₂(aq) + 6H⁺(aq) → 2Mn²⁺(aq) + 5O₂(g) + 8H₂O(l)

a) Assign oxidation states to Mn in MnO₄⁻ and Mn²⁺

b) Assign oxidation states to O in H₂O₂ and O₂

c) Identify the oxidizing agent and reducing agent

a) Mn oxidation states:
MnO₄⁻: Let x = oxidation state of Mn. x + 4(−2) = −1 → x = +7
Mn²⁺: +2

b) O oxidation states:
H₂O₂: Each O is −1 (peroxide)
O₂: Each O is 0 (elemental)

c) Agent identification:
Mn: +7 → +2 (decrease by 5) = REDUCTION → MnO₄⁻ is the oxidizing agent
O: −1 → 0 (increase) = OXIDATION → H₂O₂ is the reducing agent

Mixed Q3: Electrolysis MCQ: aqueous vs molten ▼

Compare the electrolysis of molten NaCl vs aqueous NaCl. At the anode of aqueous NaCl, why is Cl₂ produced instead of O₂?

A) Cl⁻ is always preferentially oxidized regardless of concentration
B) O₂ is never produced in aqueous electrolysis
C) Cl⁻ concentration is high enough that it is preferentially oxidized over water despite being harder to oxidize
D) The anode material determines which product forms
Correct Answer: C
Although water is thermodynamically easier to oxidize than Cl⁻ (O₂ requires lower potential than Cl₂), at high Cl⁻ concentration (like in saturated NaCl), kinetic factors dominate and Cl₂ is preferentially produced. ✓

In dilute aqueous NaCl, however, O₂ gas would form at the anode.

Why others are wrong:
A) Concentration matters significantly
B) O₂ is produced in dilute aqueous electrolysis
D) Electrode material affects rate, not this preference
Mixed Q4: Battery comparison and design ▼

A student claims that a Zn-C battery can be recharged by applying a reverse voltage. Evaluate this claim.

Evaluation: The claim is INCORRECT.

Reason 1: Zn-C batteries involve irreversible reactions. The zinc anode corrodes and is consumed during discharge.
Reason 2: Applying reverse voltage cannot regenerate the electrode materials; the reactions are not easily reversed.
Reason 3: Attempting to recharge can cause electrode damage, internal pressure buildup, and leakage of electrolyte.
Reason 4: Only batteries with reversible reactions (like lead-acid or Li-ion) can be reliably recharged.
Conclusion: Zn-C batteries are primary cells; they are designed for single-use discharge only.
Mixed Q5: Extended response: voltaic cell design ▼

Design a voltaic cell to produce electrical current from the reaction: Cu(s) + 2Ag⁺(aq) → Cu²⁺(aq) + 2Ag(s). Describe all components and explain how each contributes to function.

Model Cell Design (IB Markscheme-style):

Component 1 — Anode (−): Copper electrode. Cu is oxidized (Cu → Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻), losing electrons. These electrons are the source of electrical current.
Component 2 — Cathode (+): Silver electrode. Ag⁺ is reduced here (Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag), accepting electrons from the anode via the external circuit.
Component 3 — Half-cell solutions: One containing Cu²⁺/Cu and one containing Ag⁺. These provide the ions necessary for the redox reactions.
Component 4 — Salt bridge: Allows anions to flow from the Ag⁺ half-cell to the Cu half-cell (and cations flow in reverse), maintaining electrical neutrality. Without it, charge imbalance would stop the reaction.
Component 5 — External circuit: Connects the anode and cathode, allowing electrons to flow from Cu → Ag, driving current and powering devices.
Overall result: Spontaneous electron transfer produces measurable electrical potential (EMF) between electrodes.
