## Video 1: R3.3 — Radicals & Radical Substitution
**Due: April 6 | Audience: ALL students (SL + HL)**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide pages 61–62 (R3.3.1–3.3.3), Kognity "Reactions of Alkanes" PDF

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create an engaging audio overview for IB Chemistry students covering Reactivity 3.3 — Electron Sharing Reactions (Radicals). This is a flipped classroom assignment, so students will listen to this BEFORE class. Keep the tone conversational but accurate — like two chemistry tutors explaining to a smart high school student.
>
> Cover these three syllabus points in order:
>
> **R3.3.1 — What is a radical?**
> - A radical is a molecular entity with an unpaired electron
> - Radicals are highly reactive because of this unpaired electron
> - Give examples: •CH₃ (methyl radical) and Cl• (chlorine radical)
> - Explain that radicals can be atoms, molecules, cations, or anions
>
> **R3.3.2 — How are radicals formed? Homolytic fission.**
> - Radicals are produced by homolytic fission — the equal splitting of a bonding pair
> - This requires UV light or heat (photochemical process)
> - Use a single-barbed arrow (fish hook) to show the movement of ONE electron
> - Connect to real-world: CFCs in the atmosphere break down under UV to release chlorine radicals, which destroy ozone (O₃) but not O₂ — what does this tell us about bond strengths?
>
> **R3.3.3 — Radical substitution of alkanes**
> - Alkanes are generally unreactive (strong C-C and C-H bonds, non-polar)
> - But they DO react with halogens in the presence of UV light via radical substitution
> - Walk through the three stages using methane + chlorine as the example:
>   1. **Initiation**: Cl₂ → 2Cl• (UV breaks the Cl-Cl bond)
>   2. **Propagation**: Cl• + CH₄ → •CH₃ + HCl, then •CH₃ + Cl₂ → CH₃Cl + Cl•
>   3. **Termination**: Any two radicals combine (Cl• + Cl•, •CH₃ + Cl•, •CH₃ + •CH₃)
> - Emphasize: propagation steps regenerate a radical — that's why it's a chain reaction
> - Note that a mixture of products forms (CH₃Cl, CH₂Cl₂, CHCl₃, CCl₄)
>
> End by asking students to think about: Why are alkanes described as "kinetically stable but thermodynamically unstable"? They'll discuss this in class.

## Video 2: R3.2.15–3.2.16 — Electrolysis of Aqueous Solutions & Electroplating
**Due: April 6 | Audience: HL students ONLY**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide page 61 (R3.2.15–3.2.16), Kognity electrode potentials PDFs

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create an audio overview for IB Chemistry HL students covering two electrolysis topics. These students have already studied electrolytic cells with molten salts (R3.2.8), so build on that foundation. Keep it focused and efficient — this is an HL extension, so assume strong prior knowledge.
>
> **R3.2.15 — Electrolysis of aqueous solutions**
> - The key challenge: in aqueous solutions, WATER can also be oxidized or reduced, creating competing reactions at each electrode
> - At the cathode (reduction): either the metal ion OR water can be reduced
>   - 2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂ + 2OH⁻ (E° = -0.83 V)
>   - Compare with the metal's standard electrode potential to predict which is reduced
> - At the anode (oxidation): either the anion OR water can be oxidized
>   - 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (E° = +1.23 V)
> - Use standard electrode potentials to predict products
> - Work through TWO key examples the IB expects:
>   1. **NaCl(aq)**: Cathode = H₂ (Na is too reactive), Anode = Cl₂ (concentrated Cl⁻ is preferentially oxidized over water — note the concentration effect!)
>   2. **CuSO₄(aq)**: Cathode = Cu (Cu²⁺ is more easily reduced than water), Anode = O₂ (sulfate ions are too stable)
> - Mention that concentration and electrode material can affect outcomes
>
> **R3.2.16 — Electroplating**
> - Electroplating = using electrolysis to coat an object with a thin metallic layer
> - The object to be plated is the CATHODE
> - The plating metal is the ANODE (it dissolves)
> - The electrolyte contains ions of the plating metal
> - Students should be able to write electrode equations for the process
> - Example: silver-plating a spoon — Ag anode dissolves (Ag → Ag⁺ + e⁻), Ag⁺ deposits on the cathode spoon (Ag⁺ + e⁻ → Ag)
>
> Remind students they'll have a brief consolidation in class on April 6 to apply these concepts.

## Video 3: R3.4.4–3.4.5 — Electrophiles & Electrophilic Addition
**Due: April 8 | Audience: ALL students (SL + HL)**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide pages 62–63 (R3.4.4–3.4.5)

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create an audio overview for IB Chemistry students on electrophiles and electrophilic addition reactions. Students have just learned about nucleophiles and nucleophilic substitution in class, so this is the "other side of the coin."
>
> **R3.4.4 — Electrophiles**
> - An electrophile is a reactant that forms a bond by ACCEPTING both bonding electrons from its reaction partner (the nucleophile)
> - Electrophiles are electron-deficient — they're attracted to areas of high electron density
> - Can be neutral (e.g., Br₂ near a double bond becomes polarized) or positively charged (H⁺, NO₂⁺)
> - Compare and contrast with nucleophiles: nucleophiles DONATE electron pairs, electrophiles ACCEPT them
>
> **R3.4.5 — Electrophilic addition to alkenes**
> - Alkenes are susceptible to electrophilic attack because of the high electron density of the C=C double bond
> - The pi electrons attract electrophiles
> - These reactions are ADDITION reactions (two reactants → one product, nothing leaves)
> - Cover the reactions of alkenes with:
>   1. **Water** (hydration): alkene + H₂O → alcohol (requires acid catalyst)
>   2. **Halogens** (e.g., Br₂): alkene + Br₂ → dihalogenoalkane (this is the bromine water decolorization test!)
>   3. **Hydrogen halides** (e.g., HBr): alkene + HBr → halogenoalkane
> - Note for SL: the detailed mechanisms will NOT be assessed, but understanding the products and why alkenes react is essential
>
> Ask students to think about: What makes alkenes more reactive than alkanes? How does this connect to what you learned about radicals and alkanes in R3.3?

## Video 4: R3.4.6–3.4.8 — Lewis Acids/Bases & Complex Ions
**Due: April 8 | Audience: HL students ONLY**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide pages 63–64 (R3.4.6–3.4.8)

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create an audio overview for IB Chemistry HL students covering Lewis acid-base theory and complex ions. This extends the Brønsted-Lowry theory they already know from R3.1. Be precise with definitions — HL students need exam-ready language.
>
> **R3.4.6 — Lewis acids and bases**
> - A Lewis acid is an electron-pair ACCEPTOR
> - A Lewis base is an electron-pair DONOR
> - This is a broader definition than Brønsted-Lowry — all Brønsted-Lowry bases are Lewis bases, but not all Lewis acids are Brønsted-Lowry acids
> - Apply to both inorganic AND organic examples:
>   - BF₃ accepting a lone pair from NH₃
>   - H⁺ accepting a lone pair (connects to Brønsted-Lowry)
>   - AlCl₃ in organic reactions
> - Key insight: nucleophiles ARE Lewis bases, and electrophiles ARE Lewis acids — this ties together all of R3.4
>
> **R3.4.7 — Coordination bond formation**
> - When a Lewis base donates an electron pair to a Lewis acid, a coordination (dative) bond forms
> - Draw and interpret Lewis formulas showing this bond formation
> - Example: NH₃ donating to BF₃ → NH₃·BF₃
>
> **R3.4.8 — Complex ions**
> - Coordination bonds form when ligands donate electron pairs to transition metal cations
> - Ligands are Lewis bases (e.g., H₂O, NH₃, Cl⁻, CN⁻)
> - Students must be able to deduce the charge on a complex ion from the formula
> - Example: [Cu(H₂O)₆]²⁺ — six water ligands around Cu²⁺
> - Example: [CoCl₄]²⁻ — four Cl⁻ ligands around Co²⁺
>
> End by connecting everything: "In R3.4, you're seeing three types of bond-breaking and bond-forming — radical (homolytic), nucleophilic (heterolytic, nucleophile attacks), and electrophilic (heterolytic, electrophile attacks). Lewis theory unifies them all."

## Video 5: R3.4.9–3.4.10 — SN1/SN2 Mechanisms & Leaving Groups
**Due: April 15 | Audience: HL students ONLY**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide page 64 (R3.4.9–3.4.10), "Reactions of Alcohols and Halogenoalkanes" PPTX content

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create an audio overview for IB Chemistry HL students on nucleophilic substitution mechanisms. This is one of the most important mechanism topics in the HL course. Students already understand the basic concept of nucleophilic substitution from R3.4.2 (SL level) — now they need the mechanistic detail.
>
> **R3.4.9 — SN1 and SN2 mechanisms**
> - These are reactions between halogenoalkanes and nucleophiles (e.g., OH⁻)
> - **SN2 (primary halogenoalkanes)**:
>   - One-step, concerted mechanism
>   - Nucleophile attacks from the back side as the leaving group departs
>   - Rate = k[halogenoalkane][nucleophile] — it's bimolecular
>   - Results in inversion of configuration (stereospecific)
>   - The transition state has 5 groups around the central carbon
> - **SN1 (tertiary halogenoalkanes)**:
>   - Two-step mechanism
>   - Step 1: Leaving group departs → carbocation intermediate forms (slow, rate-determining)
>   - Step 2: Nucleophile attacks the carbocation (fast)
>   - Rate = k[halogenoalkane] — it's unimolecular
>   - NOT stereospecific (nucleophile can attack from either side)
>   - Steric hindrance around tertiary carbon prevents SN2
> - Secondary halogenoalkanes: BOTH mechanisms can occur
> - Students should be able to draw full mechanisms with curly arrows
>
> **R3.4.10 — The leaving group**
> - The rate of substitution depends on the leaving group identity
> - Better leaving groups = weaker bases = more stable when they leave
> - Order: I⁻ > Br⁻ > Cl⁻ (iodide is the best leaving group)
> - This relates to bond strength: C-I is weakest, so it breaks most easily
> - F⁻ is a very poor leaving group (C-F bond is very strong)
>
> Encourage students to practice drawing these mechanisms by hand before class — they'll be doing mechanism problems on April 15.

## Video 6: R3.4.13 — Electrophilic Substitution of Benzene
**Due: April 17 | Audience: HL students ONLY**
**Sources to upload:** IB Chemistry Guide page 64 (R3.4.13)

### NotebookLM Prompt:

> Create a focused audio overview for IB Chemistry HL students on the electrophilic substitution of benzene. This is the final new content topic of the year, so make it count. Students already understand electrophilic addition to alkenes — this builds on that but with a twist.
>
> **R3.4.13 — Electrophilic substitution of benzene**
> - Benzene is highly unsaturated (C₆H₆) but does NOT undergo addition reactions like alkenes — why?
> - The delocalized pi system is very stable. Addition would destroy this stability.
> - Instead, benzene undergoes SUBSTITUTION — one H atom is replaced by an electrophile, preserving the aromatic ring
> - The mechanism:
>   1. A strong electrophile (E⁺) attacks the delocalized pi electrons
>   2. An intermediate carbocation forms (the ring temporarily loses aromaticity)
>   3. A proton (H⁺) is lost to restore the aromatic system
> - Key example: **Nitration** — benzene reacts with a mixture of concentrated HNO₃ and H₂SO₄
>   - The electrophile is NO₂⁺ (nitronium ion), generated by: HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → NO₂⁺ + HSO₄⁻ + H₂O
>   - Connect this to Lewis acid-base theory from R3.4.6 — H₂SO₄ acts as a Lewis acid
> - Note: the IB says "the formation of the electrophile will not be assessed" — but understanding the mechanism of the ring attack IS required
> - Students should draw the mechanism showing the electrophile attacking the ring, the intermediate, and the loss of H⁺
>
> End with: "This is a beautiful example of how structure determines reactivity. Benzene's stability makes it resist addition but allows substitution — the ring always wins."

## Tips for Using These in NotebookLM

1. **Upload sources first** — the relevant IB Guide pages and any Kognity PDFs. NotebookLM works best when it can ground its explanations in your specific source material.

2. **Generate the Audio Overview** — use the "Audio Overview" feature. The prompts above go in the customization field.

3. **Length**: Each podcast-style audio should be 8–15 minutes. If it's running longer, ask NotebookLM to be more concise.

4. **Post-listening check**: Pair each audio with a 3-question Schoology quiz to verify students actually engaged with the content. Suggested question types:
   - One definition/recall question
   - One "explain why" question
   - One "predict the product" or "draw the mechanism" question

5. **Accessibility**: Consider also posting the NotebookLM study guide (text version) alongside the audio for students who prefer reading.
